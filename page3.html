Tolkien's Influence on Rowling

J.K. Rowling's Harry Potter series is another example of modern fantasy drawing inspiration from the world of The Lord of the Rings. Tolkien based his story around the general concept of the "Hero's Journey", a literary technique that takes the protagonist of the story through various developments and phases in the story. Frodo Baggins, the protagonist of Tolkien's main work, goes through the "journey" by first being offered a quest and denying the responsibilities, then accepting the quest and following through. During the quest, Frodo loses his mentor for most of the story, reaches a climactic conflict with an antagonist, and everything comes down to a resolution in which he learns a story. Harry Potter, the protagonist and titular character of Rowling's saga goes through his exact sequence as well, with a less obvious denial of responsibilities in the form of pure shock that he is actually a wizard. This is not only similarity between the two works regarding the protagonist. Tolkien specifically created the hobbits to signify the underdogs, quite literally the "little people" in life. Rowling does something similar by making her protagonist not only a child, but an orphan who barely knows anything about the world he belongs to. Both Frodo and Harry start off with the disadvantage of being isolated and "small", and ending up victorious in the end. In Sharon Black's journal piece in Children's Literature in Education, she brings up the idea of the "child of destiny", or the "child hero". Although Frodo is not quite a child when his journey beings (being a hobbit in his mid-fifties by the time he leaves for his adventure), he still has the innocence about the world he lives in compared to the much older races such as elves and dwarves, so this concept applies to him as well.
